Section 1

Introduction — What is BEEDPORTAL?

Slide: Project title slide — BEEDPORTAL logo, tagline “Plan Smart. Teach Better.”

Good morning/afternoon, members of the committee and esteemed faculty.

Today I'm presenting BEEDPORTAL — a digital teaching toolkit built specifically for Bachelor of Elementary Education students at CSAV Victoria's.

BEEDPORTAL is a web-based platform that helps BEED students create DepEd-aligned lesson plans, build teaching demonstration outlines, and organise their practicum materials — all in one place.

The core idea is simple: give our BEED students a purpose-built tool that mirrors exactly what they need during field study and practicum, so they spend less time formatting documents and more time practicing how to teach.

Open with the problem first, not the product name. The committee cares about why this exists. Keep the intro under 90 seconds — you'll demonstrate specifics in the walkthrough.

Section 2

The Problem — What BEED Students Face

Slide: 2–3 bullet points showing pain points (optional: a photo of disorganised papers or a stressed student).

Before BEEDPORTAL, our students faced a handful of recurring challenges:

First — template fragmentation. Every cooperating teacher asks for a slightly different format. Students would download DLPs from random sources online — some correct, some outdated, some not DepEd-compliant at all.

Second — no central workflow. Lesson planning, demo outlines, and teaching materials lived in separate folders, separate Word documents, with no consistency. A student might plan a demo with one structure and write their DLP in a completely different format.

Third — wasted time on formatting. Our students told us they were spending 30–40% of their planning time just getting the document layout right — the table of contents, the margins, the section headers — instead of thinking about what they would actually teach.

Fourth — no reuse. Every lesson plan was built from scratch. If a student wrote a great DLP for Math in Week 3, they had no way to adapt it for Science in Week 5 without starting over.

This section establishes why the project matters. Use real stories if you have them — even one student quote carries more weight than three bullet points. Keep it to 2 minutes.

Section 3

The Solution — BEEDPORTAL Overview

Slide: BEEDPORTAL hero section of the website, showing the headline and key value props.

BEEDPORTAL solves these problems in three targeted ways:

One — guided templates. Instead of hunting for the right format, students open BEEDPORTAL and choose from pre-built DepEd-aligned templates — 4A's, 5E's, standard DLP — each with the correct section structure already in place.

Two — integrated tools. The Demo Maker, Lesson Plan Planner, and Smart Templates live in one interface. A student can plan a demo, then immediately export a full DLP, using the same content across both tools.

Three — save and reuse. Every lesson plan a student creates becomes a personal template they can duplicate, adapt for a different subject, or export as a print-ready document.

The result: less time formatting, more time practicing. A task that used to take 90 minutes can now be done in 15–20.

Show the landing page hero here — the navy-and-gold visual hook. Let them see the product before you explain it. Point to the tagline and the three value badges on screen.

Let me walk through the three main tools:

Demo Maker. Students build step-by-step teaching demonstration plans using subject-specific guided templates. The structure walks them from motivation through presentation, discussion, and evaluation — following the 4A's or 5E's framework depending on what the lesson requires.

Lesson Plan Planner. This is the core tool. Students create complete DepEd Detailed Lesson Plans with every required section — Subject Matter, Procedure A through F, Evaluation, and Assignment — pre-structured and guided. They simply fill in their content.

Smart Templates. Once a student completes a lesson plan, they can save it as a personal template. Next time they need to plan for a different subject or grade level, they duplicate and adapt — not start from zero.

Collectively, these tools cover all 7 core subjects across Grades K–6, matching the BEED curriculum at CSAV Victoria's.

Pause after the stat cards — let the numbers sink in. The committee appreciates scope quantified. If you have usage data (even pilot data), insert it here. Spend 2.5 minutes on features.

Section 5

Design & Visual Rationale

Slide: The BEEDPORTAL colour palette (navy swatch, gold swatch, cream bg) + type specimens (Playfair Display, Inter).

The visual design was a deliberate choice. I want to briefly explain the thinking behind it, because it directly supports the product's purpose.

Navy primary — the deep blue feels academic, serious, and trustworthy. It's a colour associated with institutions and professionalism. When a BEED student opens BEEDPORTAL to prepare a practicum requirement, the visual language says "this is important work."

Gold accent — the gold is warm and aspirational. It signals quality, achievement, and the value of the teaching profession. We use it sparingly — one decisive accent per screen — so it never overwhelms.

Playfair Display for headings — a serif typeface that brings editorial gravitas. The headings feel like a publication, not a generic web app. This matters because lesson planning is a craft.

Inter for body — clean, highly readable, and system-native. Students working on their plans for hours won't experience eye strain.

The cream background — it is softer than pure white, reducing glare during long planning sessions. It also evokes the paper of a real lesson plan document.

The animations — scroll reveal, card tilt, the typewriter — are not decorative. They create a sense of discovery as the user scrolls through the product information. The motion is purposeful, not ornamental.

This is where you show design thinking. Committees want to know you made intentional choices, not just "it looked nice." Frame everything as having a rationale. Keep to 2 minutes — don't get lost in typography details.

Section 7

Technical Architecture

Slide: Architecture diagram or bullet list of tech stack.

On the technical side, BEEDPORTAL is built as a single-page application — meaning the entire experience loads once, and switching between views (landing, login, register) happens instantly without page reloads.

The tech stack:

  • HTML + CSS + vanilla JavaScript — no framework dependencies; the site is lightweight and loads fast even on slower connections common in provincial areas.
  • Tailwind CSS — used for utility-first styling, keeping the CSS clean and maintainable.
  • Custom CSS animations — scroll reveal, parallax, typewriter, card tilt, and kenBurns carousel effects are all hand-coded for performance.
  • Intersection Observer API — powers the scroll-triggered reveals and count-up animations without third-party libraries.
  • Canvas API — the particle network on the auth screens uses the HTML5 canvas for a lightweight interactive background.
  • Spring-physics scroll progress — the progress bar at the top uses a lerp-based spring simulation for smooth, natural motion.

The site is fully responsive — it works on desktop, tablet, and mobile — which matters because students may access it from their phones between classes.

It's also ready for Vercel deployment as a static site, making hosting straightforward.

Adjust depth here based on your audience. If the committee is mostly education faculty, keep it high-level and emphasise reliability, speed, and accessibility. If there are IT/computing faculty present, have a second slide with more technical detail ready to pull up.

Section 8

Anticipated Questions & Responses

Slide: Optional — just a title slide reading "Questions & Discussion"

How does this differ from existing lesson plan templates online?
BEEDPORTAL is not just a template — it's a workflow. Existing templates are static Word/PDF files. BEEDPORTAL provides guided input, saves student work, supports reuse and adaptation across subjects, and integrates demo planning with DLP writing. The templates are pre-aligned to DepEd standards, removing the guesswork.
Is this only for CSAV Victoria's students, or can it scale?
It was built for CSAV as a pilot, but the architecture is content-driven — expanding to other institutions would mean adding their subject lists and specific requirements, not rewriting the platform.
How do you ensure the lesson plans are actually DepEd-compliant?
The template structures follow the official DepEd Detailed Lesson Plan format — Subject Matter, Procedure A–F, Evaluation, Assignment — as specified in DepEd Order 42, s. 2016 and related guidelines. The 4A's and 5E's templates reflect current BEED pedagogy.
What about assessment? Can the tool grade or evaluate plans?
Not in version 1.0. BEEDPORTAL currently focuses on creation — getting the plan written in the correct format. Peer review, faculty feedback, and rubric-based assessment are logical next features but are not yet implemented.
Is there a cost for students?
The site is free for all BEED students. The goal is to remove barriers — financial and technical — so every student can produce quality lesson plans regardless of their background.
How will students learn to use it?
The interface is designed to be intuitive — sections are labelled in plain language, the process has only three steps, and form fields guide the user. A brief orientation during the Practicum Orientation event (already listed on the site) would be sufficient.
What are your plans for future development?
Version 2.0 would prioritise: (1) a personal dashboard where students see all their saved plans, (2) faculty review and commenting, (3) PDF export with proper formatting, and (4) expansion to include field study documentation forms (FS 1–6).

For each question, do not rush to answer. Repeat the question to confirm understanding, then pause before responding. If you don't know the answer, say "That's a great question — I don't have a definitive answer right now, but I'd be happy to explore it." Never bluff.
